How to set up parental controls on an iPhone or iPad

Everything lives in one place — Screen Time — and takes about 10 minutes to configure properly.

Updated June 2026 · Applies to iOS 17 and later

Before you start: use Family Sharing

You can set a Screen Time passcode directly on a child's device, but the much better setup is Family Sharing: your kid gets their own Apple ID (Apple lets you create child accounts for kids under 13), and you control everything remotely from your phone. Settings → [your name] → Family Sharing → Add Member.

Step-by-step: Screen Time for a child's device

  1. Open Screen Time. On your phone: Settings → Family → select your child → Screen Time. (Directly on the kid's device: Settings → Screen Time → "This is My Child's iPhone.")
  2. Set Downtime. This is the "phone goes to sleep" schedule — e.g. 9:00 PM to 7:00 AM. During Downtime only apps you explicitly allow (Phone, and anything in "Always Allowed") will work.
  3. Set App Limits. Add a limit for categories (e.g. Games: 1 hour/day, Social: 30 min/day) or individual apps. Kids can request more time; the request pops up on your phone.
  4. Turn on Communication Safety. Settings → Screen Time → Communication Safety. This detects nudity in Messages, AirDrop, and FaceTime and warns the child before viewing/sending. It's on-device and private — turn it on.
  5. Configure Content & Privacy Restrictions. This is the big one:
    • iTunes & App Store Purchases → require your approval for installs ("Ask to Buy").
    • Content Restrictions → Web Content → "Limit Adult Websites" (or allow-list only for younger kids).
    • Content Restrictions → set age ratings for apps, movies, TV, and music.
    • Disable changes to passcode, account, and cellular data so settings can't be undone.
  6. Set a Screen Time passcode that is different from the device passcode and that your kid does not know. Don't use a birthday.
The bypass parents miss: kids commonly dodge Screen Time by changing the device clock, deleting and reinstalling apps, or using Safari web versions of blocked apps. Fix: block app deletion (Content & Privacy → iTunes & App Store Purchases → Deleting Apps → Don't Allow), restrict date/time changes, and web-filter the browser too.

What built-in controls don't do

Screen Time is genuinely good and free — use it. But it won't monitor what's said in texts and social apps (bullying, predators, self-harm signals), it doesn't follow your kid onto a Windows PC or Android tablet, and its alerts are limited.
