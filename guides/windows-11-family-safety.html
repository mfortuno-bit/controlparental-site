How to set up parental controls on a Windows 11 PC

Microsoft Family Safety is free, built in, and decent — once you know its two big catches.

Updated June 2026 · Applies to Windows 10 & 11

How it works

Windows parental controls are tied to Microsoft accounts, not the PC itself. You create a family group, add your child's account to it, and your kid signs in to Windows with that account. You then manage everything from family.microsoft.com or the Family Safety mobile app — including remotely from your phone.

Step-by-step

  1. Create your family group. Go to family.microsoft.com, sign in with your own Microsoft account, and choose "Add a family member" → "Member" (child). Create a new account for your kid if they don't have one.
  2. Add the child account to the PC. On the computer: Settings → Accounts → Family → the child appears automatically once they're in your group. Have your kid sign in to Windows with that account — not a local account and not yours.
  3. Set screen time. On the family dashboard, open your child → Screen time. You can set per-day schedules and total daily limits, for Windows and Xbox together or separately. Kids can send "more time" requests to your phone.
  4. Filter apps and games. Child's page → Apps & games → set an age limit (e.g. "apps rated 12+ need approval"). Every install from the Microsoft Store outside the rating asks you first. You can also block specific apps.
  5. Turn on web filtering. Child's page → Edge → "Filter inappropriate websites." Add specific sites to block or allow. For younger kids, switch to "Only use allowed websites" (allow-list mode).
  6. Review activity reporting. Turn on activity reports to get a weekly email of searches, sites, apps, and screen time. Use it as a conversation starter, not a surveillance log.
The two big catches:
  1. Web filtering only works in Microsoft Edge. Family Safety automatically blocks Chrome and Firefox for filtered child accounts — but if you unblock them, filtering doesn't apply there. Leave them blocked, or use a third-party filter that covers all browsers.
  2. Local accounts bypass everything. If your kid can create or use a local Windows account (or knows your password), none of this applies. Make sure your own account has a password/PIN your kid doesn't know, and that the kid's account is a Standard user, not an Administrator.

When the built-in tool isn't enough

Family Safety covers screen time and web filtering well for a family that's all-in on Microsoft accounts and Edge. It does not monitor message content, social media, or non-Edge browsing, and game time on Steam is invisible to it.
